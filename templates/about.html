Our Mission

The fashion and textiles industry is the second largest contributor to greenhouse gas emissions in the world. It also has some of the lowest working conditions and workers rights of any sector.
Our aim is to highlight the environmental and ethical impact that different materials have. The process of manufacturing and transporting clothing and other items is complex, and it is almost impossible to trace materials from source all the way to the finished product.
Instead, we have developed a colour coded system that gives a clear indication of the impact of an item. This will allow small business owners to label their products quickly and with minimal information, and hence allow customers to shop in an eco-friendly manner. As our algorithm requires inputs that are easily found on clothing labels, customers may also check the impact of items themselves in under 30 seconds.

Along with our focus on the environmental impacts of fashion, we also care about the small businesses who work tirelessly to bring us our clothes in a sustainable way. That is why we have develped additonal tools for small business owners, so that they may keep track of their finances, advertise their business through our website, whilst also doing their part to make the world greener.


Our Methods

The CO2 equivalent for each product is derived from the material, the distance it has travelled, and its weight. The values for each material are estimates, but do give a good indication of which materials are more sustainable than others.
The ethical impact of each country was based on four measures: the minimum wage, the percentage of the population in a secure job, the corruption index, and the percentage of children who work (under 14). We chose not to include factors such as GDP, as countries with low GDP tend to rely on the fashion industry, and may have good systems of governance and adequate working conditions. For similar reasons, we also decided to give less weighting to the ethical considerations in the final impact.
These factors are combined to give a total impact on the planet and people. This impact is then divided by the mass of the product to give an overall impact per kg measure, so that different garments may be compared. A simple traffic light system is attached to this value, to aid in visual clarity while shopping. Finally, the real cost of the garment is calculated, including the cost of carbon offsetting.
